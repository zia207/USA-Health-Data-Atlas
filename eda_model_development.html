What is Spatial Statistics?

County health data live on a map, not in a spreadsheet row. Spatial statistics asks how where a county is affects the numbers you see — and whether ignoring location would mislead you. Tobler's First Law of Geography says it plainly: "Everything is related to everything else, but near things are more related than distant things."

After you build a model_frame in the Data Pipeline, use the six workflows below to explore clustering, model spatial dependence, smooth unstable small-area rates, and detect disease hot spots. Each tab runs in your browser and can export full Python code for PySAL / esda / mgwr.

  • Spatial Autocorrelation — Is the map patchy (clustered) or random?
  • Bivariate LISA — Does high X at county i co-occur with high Y in neighbouring counties?
  • Spatial Regression — Explain an outcome while accounting for spatial spillover in Y or errors.
  • Geographically Weighted — Map local correlations (GWCORR), coefficients (GW-OLS), or feature importance (GW-RF).
  • Bayesian Smoothing — Pull extreme small-county rates toward a safer regional estimate.
  • Spatial Cluster Detection — Find one statistically unusual blob of high (or low) risk.

Spatial Autocorrelation

Based on MOD07_NB02 · Moran's I & LISA

Build model_frame first, then run Global & Local Moran's I.

Explain with math (for dummies) · Spatial Autocorrelation

Plain English Look at a choropleth map of diabetes prevalence. If high counties sit next to other high counties (and low next to low), the map is spatially autocorrelated — similarity depends on location.

Weights We encode "who is a neighbor" in a matrix W. Entry wij = 1 if counties i and j share a border (queen/rook), or a positive number if they are close (distance / k-NN). Row-standardized weights make each county's neighbors sum to 1.

Global Moran's I — one number for the whole map:

I = (n / S0) · [ Σi Σj wij (yi − ȳ)(yj − ȳ) ] / [ Σi (yi − ȳ)² ]

  • I > 0 → positive autocorrelation (similar values cluster).
  • I ≈ 0 → pattern looks random.
  • I < 0 → dissimilar neighbors (checkerboard; rare in health maps).

Local Moran's I (LISA) — one score per county. Let zi = (yi − ȳ) / SD(y):

Ii = zi · Σj wij zj

  • High–High (HH) — hot spot: high county among high neighbors.
  • Low–Low (LL) — cold spot: low among low neighbors.
  • High–Low / Low–High — outlier surrounded by the opposite.

p-value We shuffle county values across the map many times (permutations) and ask: "How often does random data produce an I this extreme?" A small p-value means clustering is unlikely to be chance.

Bivariate Local Moran's I

Based on MOD07_NB09 · Bivariate LISA (LBC × poverty)

Build model_frame first, then run Bivariate Local Moran's I.

Explain with math (for dummies) · Bivariate Local Moran's I

Plain English Univariate LISA asks whether a county is high among high neighbours for the same variable. Bivariate LISA links two variables: is high mortality at county i associated with high poverty in neighbouring counties?

Bivariate Local Moran's I — one score per county:

IiXY = zX,i · Σj wij zY,j

  • zX,i = Xi − X̄ (deviation of variable X at location i).
  • zY,j = Yj − Ȳ (deviation of variable Y at neighbour j).
  • Σj wij zY,j is the spatial lag of Y around county i.

Quadrants Significant clusters use the same HH / LL / HL / LH labels, but X is at county i and the lag uses Y in neighbours:

  • High–High (HH) — high X at i, high Y in neighbours.
  • Low–Low (LL) — low X at i, low Y in neighbours.
  • High–Low / Low–High — discordant cross-variable patterns.

p-value Y values are permuted across counties many times while X stays fixed; a small p-value means the X–lag(Y) association at that location is unlikely to be chance.

Spatial Regression

OLS baseline, then SLX (lagged covariates), SAR, or SEM as needed.

Explain with math (for dummies) · Spatial Regression

Plain English Ordinary regression predicts health outcomes from risk factors. But if unmeasured nearby influences matter (shared hospitals, pollution plumes, policy regions), standard errors and coefficients can be wrong. Spatial regression models that extra "location effect."

Ordinary least squares (OLS) baseline:

y = Xβ + ε

y = outcome vector, X = predictors (poverty, smoking, …), β = coefficients, ε = leftover error. If Moran's I on ε is significant, OLS assumes independence that the map violates.

SLX — spatially lagged exogenous regressors (neighbours' covariates):

y = Xβ + WXθ + ε

(WX)i = Σj wij Xj is the weighted average of covariate X in neighbours of county i. β captures local effects; θ captures spillover from neighbouring characteristics (e.g. neighbour poverty affecting local mortality).

Spatial Lag Model (SAR / SLM) — outcome spills to neighbors (like a contagion):

y = ρ W y + Xβ + ε

ρ (rho) measures spillover: a high value in county j pulls up county i even after controlling for X. Example intuition: opioid deaths in one county affect neighboring counties.

Spatial Error Model (SEM) — hidden regional shocks in the errors:

y = Xβ + u,    u = λ W u + ε

λ (lambda) lets errors be correlated across space (unobserved regional factors), while coefficients β stay global.

Workflow here Run OLS + residual Moran's I first. If residuals cluster, try SLX (neighbours' X), SAR (lagged Y), or SEM (spatial errors). Use Compare models for an AIC table (OLS & SLX in-browser; SAR & SEM via Python export).

Geographically Weighted Models

Local Gaussian-weighted correlation, regression, or feature importance maps.

Explain with math (for dummies) · Geographically Weighted Models

Plain English Global correlation or regression assumes one relationship everywhere on the map. Geographically weighted methods let the relationship change by location — poverty may matter more in some regions than others.

GWCORR — local weighted Pearson correlation at county i:

wij = exp(−dij² / 2h²),    ri = Covi(y₁, y₂) / (σ1,i σ2,i)

Gaussian weights emphasize nearby counties; h is the bandwidth (auto-selected as 30th percentile of distances if blank).

GW-OLS — location-specific coefficients:

yi = β0(ui, vi) + Σj βj(ui, vi) Xij + εi

Each βj is estimated by weighted least squares using neighbours within the bandwidth (mgwr in Python export).

GW-RF — locally weighted random forest:

Train RFi on {(Xk, yk), wik}k=1n

At each location, samples are weighted by proximity; local feature importance maps show which predictors explain variation there.

Disease Mapping & Bayesian Smoothing

Based on MOD07_NB04 · EB & BYM mapping

Stabilise small-area rates by borrowing strength from the regional mean.

Explain with math (for dummies) · Bayesian Smoothing

Plain English A county with 2 deaths in 1,000 people can look terrifying on a map — or harmless — depending on luck. Disease mapping stabilizes noisy small-area rates by "borrowing strength" from the regional average (and optionally from neighbors).

Raw standardized mortality / incidence ratio (SMR):

SMRi = Oi / Ei

Oi = observed cases, Ei = expected cases if the county matched the national rate × population. SMR = 1 is average; SMR = 2 is double the expected rate.

Empirical Bayes (gamma–Poisson) shrinkage — a weighted average:

θ̂i = wi · SMRi + (1 − wi) · SMR̄

θ̂i is the smoothed rate. wi ∈ (0, 1) is the shrinkage weight: large populous counties keep their raw SMR (wi → 1); tiny counties get pulled toward the overall mean SMR̄ (wi → 0). The map becomes less speckled but more honest about uncertainty.

BYM model (Besag–York–Mollié) — adds spatial neighbors:

log θi = α + ui + vi,   vi ~ CAR(W),   ui ~ iid

vi is a spatially structured random effect (similar counties move together); ui is unstructured noise. In practice this produces smooth regional patterns instead of isolated outlier counties.

Read the maps Compare Raw vs Smoothed: extreme colors that disappear after smoothing were likely small-number noise, not true hot spots.

Spatial Cluster Detection

Based on MOD07_NB05 · Kulldorff scan & LISA

Kulldorff circular scan with Monte Carlo inference; compare with LISA hot spots.

Explain with math (for dummies) · Spatial Cluster Detection

Plain English LISA finds many local hot spots. A spatial scan asks a sharper question: "Is there one window on the map with surprisingly many cases, compared to what we'd expect?"

Expected cases under a constant risk:

Ei = (Σ O / Σ P) · Pi

Total observed cases O spread proportionally to each county's population Pi.

Kulldorff log-likelihood ratio (Poisson scan) for a candidate cluster C:

LLR(C) = OC log(OC/EC) + OC̄ log(OC̄/EC̄)

OC, EC = observed & expected inside the window; C̄ = outside. The algorithm grows a circle (or neighbor set) from every county and keeps the window with the largest LLR.

p-value Monte Carlo: randomly redistribute total cases across counties many times, re-run the scan each time, and count how often a random LLR ≥ your observed LLR. A small p-value means the cluster is unlikely to be chance.

LISA comparison LISA HH flags counties that are high with high neighbors — many small clusters. The scan finds the single best cluster window; use both maps together for confirmation.

Model guide (math for dummies) · All algorithms

Shared goal Predict outcome Y (e.g. overdose rate) from features X = (x₁, …, xₚ). Each model learns a function ŷ = f(X) and we score it with RMSE, MAE, or R².

OLS — Ordinary Least Squares (plain linear regression).

ŷ = β₀ + β₁x₁ + … + βₚxₚ

Pick β to minimize Σ(yᵢ − ŷᵢ)². Fast baseline; assumes a straight-line relationship.

Ridge — OLS + penalty on large coefficients (L2).

min Σ(yᵢ − ŷᵢ)² + α Σ βⱼ²

Shrinks coefficients toward zero; helps when covariates are correlated.

Lasso — OLS + L1 penalty; can zero out weak features.

min Σ(yᵢ − ŷᵢ)² + α Σ |βⱼ|

Elastic Net — mix of Ridge + Lasso.

min Σ(yᵢ − ŷᵢ)² + α [ ρΣ|βⱼ| + (1−ρ)Σβⱼ² ]

Random Forest — many decision trees on random row/feature subsamples; average their votes.

ŷ = (1/B) Σb=1B Tb(X)

Each tree asks yes/no questions (e.g. poverty > 20%?) until a leaf prediction.

Gradient Boosting — add small trees one at a time to fix previous errors.

Fm(X) = Fm−1(X) + η · hm(X),   hm fits residuals (y − Fm−1)

η = learning rate (small steps → less overfitting).

XGBoost — boosted trees with regularization (depth limit, row/column subsample, λ on leaf weights).

obj = Σ L(yᵢ, ŷᵢ) + Σ [ γT + ½λ Σ wj² ]

Similar to GBM but optimized for speed and controlled complexity.

LightGBM — gradient boosting with deeper, leaf-wise trees (grows where loss drops most).

ŷ = F0 + η Σm treem(X)

Often fast on many rows; here approximated with deeper GBM-style trees in-browser.

k-NN — predict from the k nearest counties in feature space (distance-weighted average).

ŷ = Σ wᵢ yᵢ / Σ wᵢ,   wᵢ = 1 / (dᵢ + ε)

Needs scaled features; local, non-parametric.

MLP — small neural net: hidden ReLU layer + linear output.

h = ReLU(W₁X + b₁),   ŷ = W₂h + b₂

Can capture mild non-linearity; trained with gradient descent on squared error.

SHAP (Variable Importance tab) — fair credit per feature for each prediction. Click Compute SHAP after training (sampled rows; keeps the page responsive).

φᵢ = ΣS⊆N\{i} [|S|!(|N|−|S|−1)! / |N|!] · [ v(S∪{i}) − v(S) ]

Global = mean |φ| across rows; local = waterfall for individual counties. Computed for the top 4 features by permutation importance.

Metric descriptions

  • RMSE — penalises large errors; same units as Y. Lower is better.
  • MAE — average absolute error; robust to outliers.
  • R² — variance explained; 1.0 = perfect. Higher is better.
  • MAPE — percentage error; undefined if Y = 0.
  • MedAE — median absolute error; very robust.

SHAP explainability (top 4 features)

Plain English SHAP assigns each feature a contribution φᵢ showing how much it pushed the prediction above or below the average. We rank features by permutation importance, keep the top 4, then compute exact Shapley values (all 2⁴ coalitions).

ŷ = φ₀ + φ₁ + … + φ₄   (φ₀ = baseline prediction with no feature info)

Global — mean |φᵢ| across evaluation rows (which drivers matter on average). Local — waterfall for sample rows (why this county scored high/low). County map — full evaluation-set φᵢ per FIPS for the top features.
